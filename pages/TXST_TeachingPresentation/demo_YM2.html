Road Infrastructure

  • Road infrastructure is the set of fundamental facilities and systems that support the sustainable, safe, and secure travels of general public.
  • It comprises of both public and private physical structures such as roads, railways, bridges, tunnels, waterways, and aviation.
  • Role of infrastructure is critifical for traffic safety, operation, and security.

Time mean speed (TMS)

TMS ($v_t$) is the average of all vehicles passing a point over a duration of time. It is the arithmatic mean of speeds observed at a point. TMS $v_t$ is given by,

\[\begin{aligned} v_{t}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^nv_{i} \end{aligned} \]

where $v$ is the spot speed of $i^{th}$ vehicle, and $n$ is the number of observations. In many speed studies, speeds are represented in the form of frequency table. So, TMS can be written as,

\[\begin{aligned} v_t = \frac{{\sum_{i=1}^{n}{q_i v_i}}}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}{q_i}}, \end{aligned} \]

where $q_i$ is the number of vehicles having speed $v_i$, and $n$ is the number of such speed categories.

Space Mean Speed (SMS)

SMS ($v_s$) provides spatial weightage instead of temporal. SMS is the average speed of vehicles on a road at any time. It is the harmonic mean of speeds observed at a point.

Consider a road D miles long, and let $v_i$ is the spot speed of $i_{th}$ vehicle. Let $t_i$ is the time the vehicle takes to complete D distance and is given by $\frac{D}{v_i}$. For $n$ vehicles, the average travel time $t_s$ is given by,

\[\begin{aligned} t_s = \frac{\Sigma {t_i}}{n} = \frac{1}{n}{\Sigma\frac{D}{v_i}} \end{aligned} \]

SMS can be written as,

\[\begin{aligned} v_s = \frac{D}{t_s} =\frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{1}{v_i}}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}{q_i}}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}{\frac{q_i}{v_i}}} \end{aligned} \]

where $q_i$ vehicle will have $v_i$ speed and $n$ is the number of such observations.

Traffic Volume

Number of vehicles passing a particular point of roadway during unit time. Traffic volume is expressed as vehicle/h or vehicle/day

  • Daily Volumes: Used in Highway planning
    • Average Daily Traffic (ADT): Considers day to day variation of traffic
    • Annual Average Daily Traffic (AADT): Considers seasonal variation of daily traffic within a year
  • Hourly Volumes: Used in capacity analysis
    • Reflects hourly variation of traffic within a day
  • Directional Design Hourly Volume (DDHV)
    • Used in control measures, safety and capacity. Can be compued as: $DDHV=AADT \times K \times D$, where $K$ is protion of daily traffic occuring during the peak hour and $D$ is directional distribution factor
  • Sub hourly volumes
    • Variation within a given hours is also of considerable interest, but short term peaks of flow within the peak hour may exceed the capacity.

Traffic Density (K)

Number of vehicle occupying a unit length of roadway at a given instants, usually expressed as vehivles per km

  • Difficult to measure directly. Can be computed from speed and volume.

    • \[\begin{aligned} K = \frac{Q}{\bar{V_s}} \end{aligned} \]

Congestion density

Highest density occurs when the vehicles are standstill (i.e. in jam conditions). In this case, thraffic volume approaches to zero

Headway

Space Headway (s)

Distance between fronts of successive vehicles in meters.

\[\begin{aligned} s=\frac{1000}{K}\quad Now \quad \bar{V_s} = \frac{Q}{K} \quad \rightarrow \quad \bar{V_s} = \frac{Qxs}{1000} \end{aligned} \]

Time Headway (h)

Time interval between the passage of the fronts of successive vehicles at a specific point.

\[\begin{aligned} s = \frac{h}{3600}\times \bar{V_s} \times 1000 \end{aligned} \]

Example Problem 1

Five cars are traveling with speed 40 mph, 42 mph, 51 mph, 55 mph, and 60 mph. What are the TMS and SMS?

Solution

At a point along D the TMS would be

\[\begin{aligned} \bar{u_t} = \frac{1}{N}\Sigma^{N}_{i=1}u_i&=(40+42+51+55+60)/5=49.6\: mph \end{aligned} \]

Now, average travel time

\[\begin{aligned} \bar{t} = \frac{1}{5}[\frac{D}{40}+\frac{D}{42}+\frac{D}{51}+\frac{D}{55}+\frac{D}{60}]=\frac{1}{N}\Sigma^{N}_{i=1}\frac{D}{u_i} \end{aligned} \]

SMS would be

\[\begin{aligned} \bar{u_s} = \frac{D}{\bar{t}}=\frac{D}{\frac{1}{N}\Sigma^{N}_{i=1}\frac{D}{u_i}}=\frac{1}{\frac{1}{N}\Sigma^{N}_{i=1}\frac{1}{u_i}}\\ =\frac{1}{\frac{1}{5}(\frac{1}{40}+\frac{1}{42}+\frac{1}{51}+\frac{1}{55}+\frac{1}{60})}\\ =48.4\: mph \end{aligned} \]

The TMS and SMS can be found out from the frequency table given above. First, the average speed is computed, which is the mean of the speed range. For example, for the first speed range, average speed, $v_i = \frac{2+10}{2}$ = 6 mph.

The volume of flow $q_i$ for that speed range is same as the frequency. The terms $v_i.q_i$ and $\frac{q_i}{v_i}$ are also tabulated, and their summations in the last row. Time mean speed can be computed as, $v_t = \frac{\Sigma {q_iv_i}}{\Sigma q_i} = \frac{1293}{51} = $ 25.35 mph.

Similarly, space mean speed can be computed as, $v_s = \frac{\Sigma q_i}{\Sigma \frac{q_i}{v_i}} = \frac{51}{2.65} = $ 19.23 mph.

Illustrative Example

Therefore, the headway $h_s$ is 5 sec (50/10 or 100/20). Therefore, the number of vehicles $n_s$ in an hour = 60/5 = 12 vehicles.

The density, $K_s = 1000/50$ = 20 vehicles/1000 ft. Therefore, by definition, TMS $v_t$ is given by $v_t = \frac{12\times10+12\times20}{24}$ = 15 m/s. Similarly, by definition, SMS is the mean of vehicle speeds over time. Therefore, $v_s = \frac{20\times10 + 10\times20}{30}$ = 13.3 m/s.

This is same as the harmonic mean of spot speeds obtained at location A; ie $v_s = \frac{24}{12\times\frac{1}{10}+12\times\frac{1}{20}}$ = 13.3 m/s. It may be noted that since harmonic mean is always lower than the arithmetic mean, and also as observed , space mean speed is always lower than the TMS. In other words, SMS weights slower vehicles more heavily as they occupy the road stretch for longer duration of time. For this reason, in many fundamental traffic equations, SMS is preferred over time mean speed.

Relation between TMS and SMS ($_1$)

Consider a stream of vehicles with a set of substream flow $q_1$,$q_2$, ... $q_i$, ...$q_n$ having speed $v_1$,$v_2$, ...$v_i$, ...$v_n$.

\[\begin{aligned} q = k\times v_s\quad \rightarrow \quad q_i = k_i\times v_i \end{aligned} \]

The total flow $q$ , total density $k$, and the proportion of substream density $k_i$ to the total density $k$ (i.e. $f_i$) are

\[\begin{aligned} \Sigma q_i = q\quad and \quad \Sigma k_i = k\quad and \quad f_i = \frac{k_i}{k} \end{aligned} \]

Then space mean speed and time mean speed are

\[\begin{aligned} v_s = \frac{\Sigma k_i v_i}{k}\quad and \quad v_t = \frac{\Sigma q_i v_i}{q}=\frac{\Sigma {k_i}{{v_i}^2}}{q}=\Sigma\frac{k_i}{k}v_i^2=\frac{k\Sigma {f_i}{{v_i}^2}}{q}=\frac{\Sigma {f_i}{{v_i}^2}}{v_s} \end{aligned} \]

Relation between TMS and SMS ($_2$)

Rewriting $v_t$ by adding and subtracting $v_s$,

\[\begin{aligned} v_t=\frac{\Sigma {f_i}{(v_s+(v_i-v_s))^2}}{v_s}= \frac{\Sigma {f_i}{{(v_s)^2}+{(v_i-v_s)}^2+2.v_s.(v_i-v_s)}}{v_s}\\ =\frac{\Sigma {{f_i}{v_s}^2}}{v_s} + \frac{\Sigma{f_i}{{(v_i-v_s)}^2}}{v_s} + \frac{2.v_s.{\Sigma{f_i}{(v_i-v_s)}}}{v_s} \end{aligned} \]

The third term of the equation will be zero since $v_s$ is the mean speed of $v_i$. The numerator of the second term gives the standard deviation of $v_i$. $\Sigma f_i$ by definition is 1.Therefore,

\[\begin{aligned} v_t = v_s + \frac{\sigma^2}{v_s} \end{aligned} \]

  • Time mean speed will be always greater than space mean speed since standard deviation cannot be negative.
  • If all the speed of the vehicles are the same, then spot speed, time mean speed and space mean speed will also be same.

Fundamental relations of traffic flow

  • The relationship between the fundamental variables of traffic flow, namely speed, volume, and density is called the fundamental relations of traffic flow.
  • Assume there be a road with length $v$ km, and all the vehicles are moving with $v$ km/hr. $n_1$ is the number of vehicles counted by an observer at A for one hour. The number of vehicles counted in one hour is flow(q). Therefore,

\[\begin{aligned} n_1 = q \end{aligned} \]

Fundamental diagrams of traffic flow

The relation between the density and the corresponding flow on a given stretch of road is referred to as one of the fundamental diagram of traffic flow.

  1. When the density is zero, flow will also be zero.
  2. When the number of vehicles increases, the density and flow increases.
  3. When more and more vehicles are added, it reaches a situation where vehicles can't move. This is referred to as the jam density or the maximum density where the flow will be zero.
  4. There will be some density between zero density and jam density, when the flow is maximum.


Speed-Density Diagram

  • When speed is maximum, it is referred to as the free flow speed, and when the density is maximum, the speed will be zero.
  • The most simple assumption is that this variation of speed with density is linear.
  • Corresponding to the zero density, vehicles will be flowing with free flow speed. When the density is jam density, the speed of the vehicles becomes zero.
  • It is also possible to have non-linear relationships.

Speed flow relation and Combined diagrams

  • The flow is zero either because there is no vehicles or there are too many vehicles so that they cannot move.

  • At maximum flow, the speed will be in between zero and free flow speed. The maximum flow $q_{max}$ occurs at speed $u$.

  • It is possible to have two different speeds for a given flow.

Greenshield's macroscopic stream model($_1$)

Greenshield assumed there is a linear speed-density relationship between speed and density. $v$ is the mean speed at density $k$, $v_f$ is the free speed and $k_j$ is the jam density.

\[\begin{aligned} v = v_f -\left[\frac{v_f}{k_j}\right].k \end{aligned} \]

This relation between flow and density is parabolic in shape and we know that,

\[\begin{aligned} q = k.v = v_f.k -\left [{\frac{v_f}{k_j}}\right] k^2 \end{aligned} \]

Putting $k=\frac{q}{v}$

\[\begin{aligned} q = k_j.v -\left[{\frac{k_j}{v_f}}\right] v^2 \end{aligned} \]

Greenshield's macroscopic stream model($_2$)

How to obtian the density when flow is maximum?

Differentiate therelation between flow and density with respect to $k$ and equate it to zero

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{dq}{dk}& = 0\quad \rightarrow\quad v_f - \frac{v_f}{k_j}.2k = 0\quad \rightarrow\quad k =\frac{k_j}{2} \end{aligned} \]

How to obtian the speed when flow is maximum?

Differentiate therelation between flow and speed with respect to $k$ and equate it to zero

\[\begin{aligned} q_{max}=v_f. \frac {k_j}{2}-\frac{v_f}{k_j}. \left [{\frac{k_j}{2}}\right]^2&=& v_f.\frac{k_j}{2} - v_f.\frac{k_j}{4} &=& \frac{{v_f}.{k_j}}{4} \end{aligned} \]

\[\begin{aligned} v_0& = & v_f - \frac{v_f}{k_j}.\frac{k_j}{2}& = & \frac{v_f}{2} \end{aligned} \]

It indicates that the maximum flow is one fourth the product of free flow and jam density and the speed at maximum flow is half of the free speed.

Denoting y = v and x = k, $\bar{x} =\frac{\Sigma{x}}{n} =\frac{390}{4}$ = 97.5, $\bar{y} = \frac{\Sigma{y}}{n} = \frac{85}{4}$ = 21.3, b = $\frac{-2947.7}{13157.2}$ = -0.2, $a = y - b\bar{x}$ = 21.3 + 0.2$\times$97.5 = 40.8. So the linear regression equation will be,

\[\begin{aligned} v = v_f-\frac{v_f}{k_j}k = 40.8 - 0.2 k \end{aligned} \]

This implies, $k_j = \frac{40.8}{0.2}$ = 204 veh/km. The basic parameters of Greenshield's model are free flow speed and jam density and they are obtained as 40.8 kmph and 204 veh/km respectively.

The maximum flow is $q_{max} = \frac{40.8 \times 204}{4}$ = 2080.8 veh/hr Density corresponding to the speed 30 km/hr can be found as k = $\frac{40.8 - 30}{0.2}$ = 54 veh/km
